High-bandwidth memory (HBM) has quietly become the single most expensive component in an AI chip — and its share is still climbing.

Epoch AI published a detailed cost breakdown covering chips from Nvidia, AMD, Google, and Amazon, weighted by production volume. The finding is stark: memory's share of total component spending rose from 52% in Q1 2024 to 63% by Q4 2025. In absolute terms, HBM spending across these four designers nearly tripled from roughly $12 billion to $32 billion over the same period — a faster year-over-year increase than any other component category.

Logic dies stayed roughly flat at 13-14% of component costs. Advanced packaging (TSMC CoWoS) fell from 19% to 15%, and auxiliary components like substrate and power delivery dropped from 15% to 9%.

The trend is driven by the insatiable appetite of large-scale AI training and inference for memory bandwidth. As models grow larger and context windows expand, HBM3e stacks have become a bottleneck — and a pricing leverage point for memory suppliers like SK Hynix, Samsung, and Micron. The tight supply environment is expected to persist: hyperscalers are already pricing higher component costs into their capex guidance, with Microsoft citing approximately $25 billion from higher component prices in its $190 billion FY2026 outlook and Meta raising its 2026 capex range by $10 billion.

For anyone watching the economics of AI infrastructure, this is a signal worth tracking. Memory is no longer a supporting player in chip costs — it has become the primary cost driver, and its trajectory will shape everything from model pricing to data center buildout decisions.
