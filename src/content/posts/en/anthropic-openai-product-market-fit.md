Simon Willison, former Django co-creator and long-time AI commentator, published a data-rich analysis arguing that April 2026 marks a new inflection point: Anthropic and OpenAI have finally found genuine product-market fit, and it is driven by coding agents.

The evidence is concrete. Both companies have shifted their enterprise pricing from flat-rate seat fees to API token-based consumption. Anthropic moved its Enterprise plan to $20 per seat plus API pricing late last year, while OpenAI made a parallel change in April. A power user running Claude Code burns roughly $1,200 per month in tokens at API rates — orders of magnitude more than the flat consumer subscription fees.

Willison argues that the widely cited "AI budget blowout" stories actually support, rather than contradict, the PMF thesis. Uber's CTO reportedly told investors the company maxed out its full-year AI budget within months, mostly due to Claude Code. Microsoft canceled Claude Code licenses to control spending. The classic pricing heuristic applies: if customers wince but still say yes, you have found your price point.

Perhaps the most striking data point comes from SpaceX's unexpected S-1 filing, which revealed that Anthropic has committed to paying $1.25 billion per month for inference compute through May 2029. This single vendor contract hints at the sheer scale of inference demand the major labs are operating at today.

Anthropic is now rumored to be on track for \$10.9 billion in quarterly revenue, potentially turning its first profit. The companies are hiring aggressively for enterprise sales roles — 32% of OpenAI's open positions and 27% of Anthropic's are enterprise-facing.

The deeper implication is strategic. By moving enterprise pricing to API token consumption and cutting out intermediaries like Cursor and GitHub Copilot (which previously resold Anthropic's API at a markup), the frontier labs are capturing the full value of their own infrastructure. Willison calls this the April 2026 inflection point, following the November 2025 inflection when agents first became reliably useful. The S-1 filings for the upcoming IPOs will tell us whether the revenue numbers match the narrative.
